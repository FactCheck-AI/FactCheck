Beatles history, Nov. 2:

• 1973, John Lennon’s album “Mind Games” was released in the U.S.

• 1974, George Harrison played in Vancouver, Canada. Harrison began his first tour in eight years. He was the first former Beatle to attempt a nationwide solo tour.

• 1987, George Harrison’s album “Cloud Nine” was released in the U.K. George’s 10th solo album marked a return to commercial music making after a five-year hiatus. Ringo Starr, Eric Clapton, Gary Wright, Jim Keltner and Elton John helped out, supporting Jeff Lynne’s efforts.

• 2004, John Lennon’’s “Rock N Roll” was released with four extra tracks.

• 2010, Paul McCartney’s 1973 album”Band on the Run” was reissued in a four-disc set that also included a 120-page book. Several different editions were also available.

U.S. presidency/presidential elections/cabinet/Congress/Supreme Court/U.N. history, Nov. 2:

• 1795, James Knox Polk, the 11th president of the United States, was born in Mecklenburg County, N.C.

• 1865, Warren Gamaliel Harding, the 29th president of the United States, was born near Marion, Ohio.

• 1976, Former Georgia Gov. Jimmy Carter became the first candidate from the Deep South since the Civil War to be elected president as he defeated incumbent Gerald R. Ford.

• 1989, President George H.W. Bush and congressional Republicans dropped their Capitol Hill quest for a cut in the capital gains tax.

• 1990, The White House announced that President George H.W. Bush planned to spend Thanksgiving with American soldiers in Saudi Arabia.

• 1991, The Rev. Jesse Jackson, who ran for the presidency in 1984 and 1988, announced he would not be a candidate in 1992.

• 2004, President George W. Bush was elected to a second term as Republicans strengthened their grip on Congress.

American Revolution history, Nov. 2:

• 1783, General George Washington issued his Farewell Address to the Army near Princeton, N.J.

American Civil War history, Nov. 2:

• 1859, John Brown was convicted of treason against Virginia, murder and conspiracy for his raid on Harper’s Ferry. He was hanged one month later

• 1861, President Abraham Lincoln relieved Maj. Gen. John C. Fremont of his command of the Army’s Department of the West based in St. Louis, following Fremont's unauthorized efforts to emancipate slaves in Missouri.

World War I history, Nov. 2:

• 1912, Bulgaria defeats the Ottoman Empire in the Battle of Lule Burgas, the bloodiest battle of the First Balkan War, which opens her way to Constantinople.

• 1914, The Russian Empire declares war on the Ottoman Empire and the Dardanelles are subsequently closed.

World War II history, Nov. 2:

• 1940, First day of Battle of Elaia–Kalamas between the Greeks and the Italians.

Korean War history, Nov. 2:

• 1951, A platoon of The Royal Canadian Regiment defends a vital area against a full battalion of Chinese troops in the Battle of the Song-gok Spur. The engagement lasts into the early hours the next day.

Vietnam War history, Nov. 2:

• 1963, South Vietnamese President Ngo Dihn Diem was assassinated in a military coup.

• 1965, Norman Morrison, a 31-year-old Quaker, sets himself on fire in front of the river entrance to the Pentagon to protest the use of napalm in the Vietnam War.

• 1967, U.S. President Lyndon B. Johnson and “The Wise Men” conclude that the American people should be given more optimistic reports on the progress of the Vietnam War.

Middle East/Persian Gulf/Afghanistan history, Nov. 2:

• 1917, British Foreign Secretary Arthur Balfour issued a declaration expressing support for a “national home” for the Jews in Palestine.

• 1951, More than 6,000 British troops were flown into Egypt to quell unrest in the Suez Canal zone.

• 1956, Israel occupies the Gaza Strip.

• 1964, King Saud of Saudi Arabia is deposed by a family coup, and replaced by his half-brother Faisal.

• 1986, Kidnappers in Lebanon released American hospital administrator David Jacobsen after holding him for 17 months.

• 1994, In Durunka, Egypt, more than 475 people were killed when fuel carried by floodwaters ignited.

Civil Rights/Human Rights history, Nov. 2:

• 1920, White mobs rampaged through the Florida citrus town of Ocoee, setting fire to Black-owned homes and businesses, after a Black man, Mose Norman, showed up at the polls to vote on Election Day; some historians estimate as many as 60 people were killed.

• 1983, U.S. President Ronald Reagan signed the bill establishing a national holiday to mark the birthday anniversary of civil rights leader Martin Luther King Jr.

Today’s Highlights in History:

On Nov. 2, 1899, The Boers began their 118-day siege of British-held Ladysmith in South Africa during the Second Boer War.

On Nov. 2, 1917, The Military Revolutionary Committee of the Petrograd Soviet, in charge of preparation and carrying out the Russian Revolution, holds its first meeting.

On Nov. 2, 1930, Haile Selassie was crowned emperor of Ethiopia. His reign lasted nearly 44 years.

On Nov. 2, 1936, The British Broadcasting Corporation initiates the BBC Television Service, the world’s first regular, “high-definition” (then defined as at least 200 lines) service. Renamed BBC1 in 1964, the channel still runs to this day.

On Nov. 2, 1947, Howard Hughes piloted his huge wooden flying boat, the Hughes H-4 Hercules (derisively dubbed the “Spruce Goose” by detractors), on its only flight, which lasted about a minute over Long Beach Harbor in California.

On Nov. 2, 1949, The Dutch-Indonesian Round Table Conference ends with the Netherlands agreeing to transfer sovereignty of the Dutch East Indies to the United States of Indonesia.

On Nov. 2, 1956, Hungarian Revolution: Imre Nagy requests U.N. aid for Hungary. Nikita Khrushchev meets with leaders of other Communist countries to seek their advice on the situation in Hungary, selecting János Kádár as the country’s next leader on the advice of Josip Broz Tito.

On Nov. 2, 1958, Chicago and Los Angeles establish an NFL attendance record when 90,833 fill the L.A. Coliseum to see the Rams beat the Bears 41-35.

On Nov. 2, 1959, Former game show contestant Charles Van Doren admitted to a House subcommittee that he was given questions and answers in advance when he appeared on the NBC’s “Twenty-One.”

On Nov. 2, 1960, Penguin Books is found not guilty of obscenity in the trial R v Penguin Books Ltd, the Lady Chatterley's Lover case.

On Nov. 2, 1960, Roger Maris beat teammate Mickey Mantle by three votes, 225-222, to win the second closest American League MVP vote. The closest MVP race occurred in 1947, when Joe DiMaggio edged Ted Williams by a single vote.

On Nov. 2, 1966, The Cuban Adjustment Act comes into force, allowing 123,000 Cubans the opportunity to apply for permanent residence in the United States.

On Nov. 2, 1972, Steve Carlton, a 27-game winner for the last-place 59-97 Philadelphia Phillies, was the unanimous winner of the National League Cy Young award winner. Carlton went 27-10 with a 1.97 ERA and accounted for 46 percent of the team’s wins.

On Nov. 2, 1974, The Atlanta Braves traded Hank Aaron to the Milwaukee Brewers for outfielder Dave May so Aaron could return to where he started his career at Milwaukee’s County Stadium. Aaron played two seasons for the Brewers and hit the final 22 home runs of his career total of 755.

On Nov. 2, 1979, Black militant JoAnne Chesimard escaped from a New Jersey prison, where she was serving a life sentence for the 1973 slaying of New Jersey state trooper Werner Foerster. Chesimard, who took the name Assata Shakur, is believed to be living in Cuba.

On Nov. 2, 1985, Gordon Brown has 214 yards and quarterback Steve Gage 206 to become the first teammates to each rush for more than 200 yards as Tulsa beats Wichita State 42-26. Brown transferred to Tulsa from Hutchinson (Kan.) Community College.

On Nov. 2, 1988, The Morris worm, the first Internet-distributed computer worm to gain significant mainstream media attention, is launched from MIT.

On Nov. 2, 1990, The Golden State Warriors beat the Denver Nuggets 162-158 at McNichols Arena. The 320 points set an NBA record for the most points scored by two teams in a non-overtime game.
